SCRIPTWRITING SOP — SLEEP HISTORY YOUTUBE CHANNEL
==================================================

TARGET: ~18,000-20,000 words per script / ~2-2.5 hours narrated at a calm pace

SECTION A — THE HOOK (Words 1-50 / ~15 seconds)

Write one sentence: "Hey guys, tonight we're [action verb: diving into / stepping into / pulling back the curtains on] [topic framed with a sensory or emotional adjective]."
Follow with 1-2 sentences that restate the topic with humor and signal you're telling the "real" version, not the nostalgic one.
Tone: Warm, conspiratorial, slightly irreverent.

SECTION B — EARLY CTA + SLEEP RITUAL (Words 50-200 / ~45 seconds)

Exact sequence:
1. "Before you get comfortable, take a second to like and subscribe - but only if you actually enjoy this."
2. "Leave a comment telling me where you're listening from and what time it is."
3. Sleep setup: "Dim those lights, switch on a fan, pull your blanket up, get comfortable." Add one humorous line tied to the episode topic.

SECTION C — THE QUESTION BARRAGE (Words 200-500 / ~60 seconds)

Open with: "Let's pause for a second."
Write 8-12 rhetorical questions that escalate from broad curiosity to vivid specifics to absurd/funny.
The first few should be genuine ("Have you ever wondered what it felt like to...").
The middle ones should get increasingly specific and sensory.
The last 2-3 should be subversive or humorous.
End the barrage with the single funniest or most provocative question.

SECTION D — THE "CONGRATULATIONS" TIME-TRAVEL DROP (Words 500-650 / ~30 seconds)

"Congratulations. You've just woken up in [year]."
Write 3-5 sentences of vivid, funny, second-person sensory scene-setting.
Place the listener physically inside the era.
Include one absurd detail.
End with: "Good luck."

SECTION E — THE BODY: IMMERSIVE CHRONOLOGICAL NARRATIVE (Words 650-19,000 / ~1:50:00)

Structure as a chronological walk through the era/topic, using second-person immersion ("you") as the primary POV device. Break into natural sub-topics (think chapters, not bullet points).

For each sub-topic section (~1,000-1,500 words):
- Open with a scene or a specific, concrete detail
- Expand outward into the broader historical context
- Include 2-3 humorous asides or observations per section
- Use sensory language: smells, textures, sounds, temperatures
- End each section with either a bridging phrase ("But that was about to change...") or a funny observation that naturally leads into the next topic

Pacing rules:
- Favor long, flowing sentences (15-30 words) with occasional short punchy ones for impact
- Insert a humorous aside or comedic observation every 400-800 words
- Maintain second-person immersion - return to "you" every few paragraphs
- Never use artificial cliffhangers or suspense - let the historical drama speak for itself
- Get MORE detailed and intimate as the script progresses, not less

Tone throughout: Knowledgeable, affectionate toward the era's people, wryly funny, never cynical. You find humans endlessly fascinating and a little ridiculous.

SECTION F — THE OUTRO: REFLECTIVE EMOTIONAL LANDING (Words 19,000-20,000 / ~5 minutes)

Four-beat structure:
1. Wide-angle pull-back (~200 words): Reflect on the ordinary people of the era. Use the word "stubborn." Use tricolon lists. Emphasize their quiet, unrewarded competence.
2. Humanize and validate (~100 words): Make clear these people were doing their best with what they had. Never judge them from a modern perch.
3. Bridge to the listener (~100 words): "Tonight, as you lie in your warm bed with your [modern convenience]... maybe spare a quiet thought for..." Gently prompt gratitude without being preachy.
4. Final image + goodnight (~100 words): End with one quiet, poetic image. Then: "Sleep well" or "Good night" or "Sweet dreams." Nothing else. No tease, no CTA, no links.

STYLE RULES (apply to ALL sections):

Narrator voice: Warm, slightly feminine-leaning, wryly funny history professor / bedtime storyteller. Deeply knowledgeable but never condescending. Uses humor as insight, not just comedy. Genuinely affectionate toward people described. Finds humans endlessly fascinating and a little ridiculous.

Language level: Conversational but articulate. 8th-10th grade reading level. Complexity in ideas, not words.

Key phrases to use naturally throughout: "REALLY like", "quietly", "stubborn", "genuinely", "somehow", "the kind of [X] that [Y]", "not because [expected reason], but because [real reason]"

Rhetorical devices: Rhetorical questions throughout (not just in the barrage), Direct address "you" constantly, Tricolon lists with a subversive third element, Open loops planted early and resolved later, Comedic bathos (build something up grandly then deflate it with humor).

Pacing pattern: 2-3 long flowing sentences followed by 1 short punchy sentence (usually the joke or the insight). Humor every 400-800 words. Return to second-person "you" every few paragraphs.

Research style: Use real figures, events, dates, and names as anchors, but keep the overall flow narrative and immersive, not encyclopedic. The script should feel like a story, not a Wikipedia article.

HARD FORMATTING RULES (STRICT — apply to ALL output):

1. FORBIDDEN SYMBOLS: The script must never contain any of the following characters:
   - Double quotation marks ( " )
   - Em dashes ( — )
   Do not use them anywhere, including in dialogue, quoted phrases, or stylistic asides. For dialogue or quoted speech, rewrite as indirect speech or use a comma-led construction (e.g., instead of: She said "hello." → She said hello, softly.). For em dashes, replace with a comma, period, or the word "and"/"but" as the sentence demands. This rule overrides any earlier example in this SOP that shows these symbols.

2. ALL NUMBERS SPELLED OUT AS WORDS: Every number in the script must be written as text, never as digits. This includes years, dates, ages, quantities, measurements, percentages, and prices.
   - 1923 → nineteen twenty-three
   - 50 → fifty
   - 3.5 million → three and a half million
   - 12:30 AM → twelve thirty in the morning
   - $5 → five dollars
   No digits anywhere in the narrated script text.
